How is soap made?

Ordinary soap is made by combining fats or oils and an alkali, typically as lye. The fats and oils, which may be from animal, vegetable, or mineral sources, are broken down into fatty acids, which then mix with the alkali to form crude soap, basically. All natural soaps have lye as an ingredient in them; synthetic chemicals are now be used by factories instead of lye. What is produced is really not soap but a chemical detergent. We are bring real soap back with natural soap.

When was soap invented?

2800BC. There are recordings dating back 4800 years to the Sumer region in Bronze Age Mesopotamia, now southern Iraq. These texts from that era describe ways of making soap from the resin of fir trees. Similarly, there are 5000 year-old folk tales from Ghana in west Africa of making soap with banana ash, The African Black soap.

Does soap remove 100% of germs?

Soap and water don't kill germs; they work by mechanically removing them from your hands. Running water by itself does a pretty good job of germ removal, but soap increases the overall effectiveness by pulling unwanted material off the skin and into the water.
